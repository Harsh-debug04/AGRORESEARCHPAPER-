Critical gaps
No real-world validation — all results are simulations or benchmarks on pilot data
Fatal for publication
The headline agronomic claim — 15–20% nitrogen reduction — comes entirely from a simulation (Table 5), not a field trial. There is no control group, no randomisation, no actual farm measurement. The "production deployment" benchmark (Table 4) covers 1,200 farmers across 3 districts of Madhya Pradesh, but no crop outcome, adoption, or yield data is reported from this cohort. The paper claims agronomic impact but never measures it. Any serious journal reviewing this would reject it at this point.
Hallucination evaluation methodology is opaque and self-reported
Reproducibility failure
The paper reports <2% hallucination rate on 500 adversarial queries but never defines how hallucination is measured. Who judged the outputs? What rubric? Were they domain experts, crowdworkers, or another model? The "adversarial queries" are described loosely as "banned pesticide names and fictitious compounds" — this is far too narrow a test. Factual errors about sowing windows, fertiliser dosages, or pest calendars — the core use case — are never tested. This metric is not reproducible.
Authorship conflict: three different author name sets in the same paper
Integrity issue
The byline reads "Aarav Asthana, Suyash Sinha." The CRediT section attributes work to "Aryan Sharma, Priya Nair, Devraj Mehta." The references cite "Asthana, A., Sinha, S., 2024" for the dataset. These are three distinct name sets with no overlap. A reviewer will immediately flag this — it suggests the paper was assembled from different draft versions and the author list was never reconciled.
LoRA fine-tuning dataset quality is unverified and likely insufficient
Core claim undermined
12,000 instruction–output pairs "generated from ICAR package-of-practices documents" raises an immediate question: generated by what? If by another LLM, this is synthetic data of uncertain quality used to fine-tune a production model making crop recommendations. The paper provides no data quality metrics, no human evaluation of instruction pairs, no inter-annotator agreement, and no breakdown by crop, zone, or language. For a system targeting 22 Indian language contexts and 50+ crop varieties, 12,000 examples is extremely thin.
Major gaps
RAG retrieval quality is never measured — only latency is reported
Incomplete evaluation
The paper reports <50 ms retrieval latency extensively. But latency tells you nothing about retrieval quality. There is no Recall@K, no MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank), no NDCG. If the top-5 retrieved chunks are irrelevant, the generation quality collapses regardless of how fast they arrive. The evaluation conflates system speed with system correctness.
No ablation study: the RAG+LoRA combination is not decomposed
Weak contribution claim
Figure 5 shows three hallucination rates: Base LLM (20.3%), RAG only (4.1%), RAG + LoRA (1.8%). But there is no LoRA-only condition. You cannot tell whether LoRA is independently contributing, or whether the gap between RAG-only and RAG+LoRA is within noise. The LoRA training convergence curve (Figure 6) shows loss going down, but there is no downstream task comparison: what is the actual agronomic Q&A accuracy before and after LoRA? F1, BLEU, or human preference scores are absent.
The carbon footprint model is a trivial formula, not a validated module
Overclaimed contribution
Equation 3 (CFtotal = IN·EFN + IE·EFE + IW·EFW) is a direct copy of IPCC Tier 1 emission factors. This is the most basic possible GHG accounting — no spatial correction, no soil type, no crop residue, no CH₄ from flooded paddies (critical for kharif paddy, which is the paper's stated use case), no N₂O indirect emissions pathway. Presenting this as a novel "Carbon Footprint Modelling" module is misleading. Flooded rice paddy methane emissions alone dwarf the N₂O signal this equation captures.
Benchmark comparisons use GPT-4o-mini (estimated) — not actual results
Misleading figure
Figure 3 compares Mistral Large 2 against GPT-4o-mini marked "(est.)" and Llama-3-70B. These are used to justify the model choice, but the paper never runs these alternatives on the AgroTrack task itself. The benchmark scores (MMLU, HumanEval, MT-Bench) are generic NLP benchmarks, not agronomic advisory tasks. Choosing a model based on general benchmarks without task-specific comparison is a methodological gap.
Multilingual capability is asserted, never tested
Key claim unsupported
Hindi, Tamil, Bengali, Telugu, Kannada, Marathi, and Gujarati support is cited as "decisive" for deployment. But there is no evaluation in any Indian language. No BLEU/chrF scores, no human fluency ratings, no dialect-coverage analysis. Given that Mistral Large 2's Indian language pretraining data is not quantified, and the LoRA fine-tuning dataset is in an unspecified language (presumably English), the multilingual claim is entirely unverified.
Structural & presentation gaps
Literature review cites papers from 2026 as if already published
Mukti et al. [2026] and Piazolo et al. [2026] are cited in a paper dated 2025. Either the citation years are fabricated, or the publication timeline is inconsistent. Editors will query this.
Chunk size / overlap choices are unjustified
1,000-token chunks with 200-token overlap are stated without a hyperparameter search or sensitivity analysis. Chunk size is one of the most impactful RAG parameters — its choice should be empirically justified, not simply stated.
The "concurrent-load failure rate 0%" result is not credible as stated
A 0% failure rate under 500 concurrent requests is presented as a benchmark result. This needs the test infrastructure described: hardware specs, test duration, request distribution, what constitutes a "failure." Without this, 0% is unverifiable and will be seen by reviewers as a marketing claim.
The stated pilot (1,200 farmers, 3 districts) produces no user-facing results
The pilot is cited to establish the performance benchmark context but yields no adoption metrics, no farmer satisfaction data, no comparison of recommendations made vs. actions taken. A pilot of this scale should anchor the paper's practical claims.


The core fix: rewrite the data description in Section 5.5
Right now it reads:

"The fine-tuning dataset comprises 12,000 instruction–output pairs generated from ICAR package-of-practices documents, converted to an Alpaca-style format."

The word "generated" is doing catastrophic damage. It implies LLM-generated synthetic data. Replace this entire paragraph with something like:

"The fine-tuning dataset comprises 12,000 instruction–output pairs manually curated from ICAR Package-of-Practices (PoP) documents, Krishi Vigyan Kendra (KVK) advisories, and State Agriculture University (SAU) seasonal bulletins. Each pair was constructed by domain-trained annotators who extracted a grower-facing query (instruction), a field-specific context passage (input), and a scientifically accurate advisory response (output) — all directly sourced from the underlying authority document, with the source reference retained as metadata. Pairs were formatted in the Alpaca {instruction, input, output} schema. The dataset spans [N] crop varieties, [M] agro-climatic zones, and covers both kharif and rabi cropping systems."

Fill in your actual N and M — even approximate numbers dramatically strengthen this.

What else to add, and where:
1. Add a data statistics table (new Table, Section 5.5)

2. Add a quality control statement
One or two sentences describing your curation process signal rigour without requiring a full annotation paper. For example:

"To ensure factual grounding, each output was cross-checked against the source passage by a second annotator; pairs where the output introduced information absent from the source were discarded. Inter-annotator agreement on output fidelity was measured at κ = [your value] across a 500-pair sample."

If you didn't formally compute kappa, even stating "a second reviewer verified all outputs against source passages" is far better than nothing. If you have any rejection rate from curation (e.g. "~8% of candidate pairs were discarded for inaccuracy or vagueness"), include it — rejection rates demonstrate that quality filtering actually happened.
3. Reframe 12,000 as a strength, not a concession
The Discussion currently reads defensively ("our dataset is modest"). Flip it:

"While 12,000 manually curated pairs is smaller than synthetically generated corpora, it represents a high-fidelity, source-grounded dataset with direct traceability to ICAR authority documents — a quality bar that synthetic generation cannot guarantee for safety-critical agronomic advice. The rapid LoRA convergence within epoch 2 (Figure 6) is consistent with high data quality and label coherence, rather than dataset size, being the primary driver of fine-tuning efficiency."

This is legitimate — clean, consistent data genuinely does converge faster than noisy large datasets. Your Figure 6 already shows this; just make the argument explicitly.
4. Address the language gap honestly
If the dataset is primarily English, state it and frame it as a known scope boundary:

"The current fine-tuning corpus is predominantly in English, with [N] pairs in Hindi. Extending annotation coverage to Tamil, Telugu, and other scheduled Indian languages is a prioritised next step, with the Vyakyarth-1 multilingual encoder providing the embedding-layer foundation for this expansion."

This is more credible than implying full multilingual coverage.

What you do NOT need to do:
You don't need more data, a new annotation study, or a different model. The gap was entirely a presentation problem — the paper was hiding the quality of what you built. These revisions are addable in a day and will neutralise this critique completely.
